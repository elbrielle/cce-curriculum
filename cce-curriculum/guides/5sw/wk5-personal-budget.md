## Lesson Objective

Students will complete the Hats & Ladders MoneySkills budgeting module, create a personal budget based on a real DFW salary from a career they explored, compare the cost of living across cities, use NGPF or EverFi financial literacy resources to deepen understanding, and update their Career Plan with A&C cluster wrap-up data.

## Demonstration of Learning

> "I can create a personal budget based on a realistic DFW salary, explain the difference between needs and wants, and compare cost of living across at least two cities to understand how location affects financial planning."

## TEKS Alignment

- **d(5)(D):** Prepare a personal budget that reflects the desired lifestyle.
- **d(5)(E):** Use resources to compare salaries of at least three careers in the student's interest area.
- **d(5)(A):** Analyze labor market information such as job availability and economic conditions.
- **d(3)(C):** Identify methods available to pay for education and training after high school such as FAFSA, scholarships, grants, and work-study.

## Career Connection

Financial literacy is one of the most practical skills students will ever learn. Understanding how to budget, save, and plan financially is essential regardless of career path. This week connects directly to students' career exploration: they will choose a career they have explored this year, use its REAL DFW salary, and build a budget showing whether that salary can support the lifestyle they want. The H&L MoneySkills module provides the foundation, and NGPF/EverFi provide additional depth. This is the primary d(5)(D) week of the entire course.

**Irving ISD Pathway: This week applies to ALL Irving ISD pathways. Financial literacy is a universal career skill. Students use salary data from careers they explored across all clusters to build their personal budget.**

## Vocabulary

- **Personal Budget:** A plan for how you will spend and save your money over a specific time period, usually monthly.
- **Net Income:** The amount of money you take home after taxes and other deductions are subtracted from your gross (total) pay.
- **Fixed Expenses:** Costs that stay the same each month, like rent, car payments, and insurance.
- **Variable Expenses:** Costs that change from month to month, like groceries, gas, entertainment, and clothing.
- **Cost of Living:** The amount of money needed to cover basic expenses (housing, food, transportation, healthcare) in a specific location. Cost of living varies significantly between cities.

## Bridge to Theory (Hats & Ladders)

The H&L MoneySkills module is the anchor activity for this week. MoneySkills teaches students about budgeting through interactive activities where they allocate income across expense categories based on realistic career salaries. The module connects directly to the careers students have been exploring all year -- they can see what life actually COSTS on a given salary. After completing MoneySkills, students supplement with NGPF or EverFi financial literacy content and then build their own personalized budget using real DFW data.


## IISD Instructional Strategies

- **Modeling:** Teacher models a complete personal budget on the projector using a real DFW salary (e.g., electrician at $52,000/year). Walk through calculating monthly net income, then allocating to rent, utilities, food, transportation, insurance, savings, and discretionary spending. Students see the 'math of real life' before doing it themselves.
- **Think-Pair-Share:** After completing their budgets, students compare results with a partner who chose a DIFFERENT career salary. They discuss: Who has more money left over? Why? What trade-offs did each person make?

## Lesson Sequence

### Day 1: H&L MoneySkills Module

**WARM-UP: If you earned $3,000 per month AFTER taxes, how would you split it up? Write down your best guess for rent, food, transportation, and fun money.**

#### H&L MoneySkills (35 min)

> **Teacher:** "This week is about MONEY. Specifically, can you live the life you want on the salary your dream career pays? Hats and Ladders has a module called MoneySkills that teaches you budgeting through interactive activities. Open it up and let us see what real life costs."

> [H&L PLATFORM] Note: The H&L workbook does not include a standalone "MoneySkills" budgeting module. However, the workbook's "Lifestyle Snapshot" activity (Ch 16: My Next Steps) has students consider career salary and lifestyle planning. Use the app's budgeting features if available, or supplement with the teacher-led personal budget activity below using salary data from the Hat Finder. Students can pull localized DFW salary data from their favorited careers in H&L to create a realistic personal budget.

#### Debrief Discussion (10 min)

> **Teacher:** "What surprised you? Did anyone realize they could not afford the lifestyle they wanted on their career's salary? What did you have to cut? This is EXACTLY why career planning and financial planning go hand in hand."

**DOK 2: How would you describe the difference between 'needs' and 'wants' based on what you learned in the MoneySkills module?**

**EXIT TICKET: Name one thing you learned about budgeting from the H&L MoneySkills module that surprised you. (d(5)(D))**

### Day 2: Building My Personal Budget

**WARM-UP: What is the most expensive thing your family pays for every month? Make a guess.**

#### Teacher Budget Modeling (15 min)

> **Teacher:** "Watch my screen. I am going to build a complete personal budget using a real DFW salary. I will use an electrician's salary -- about $52,000 per year. First, I need to calculate my monthly take-home pay after taxes. Then I will allocate money to each expense category. Watch what happens when I try to add a car payment AND student loans at the same time."

Teacher models a complete budget on the projector: gross salary to net income calculation (estimate 25% for taxes), then allocation to housing (aim for 30% or less), utilities, food, transportation, insurance, savings, debt, and discretionary spending.

#### Student Budget Creation (25 min)

> **Teacher:** "Now your turn. Pick a career you explored this year. Use its DFW salary from H&L or BLS. Calculate your monthly net income, then fill in the budget template. Be realistic -- look up actual DFW apartment rent, car insurance costs, and grocery estimates."

Students choose a career and build their own budget using the printed Personal Budget Template. They use H&L salary data and research real DFW costs: average rent ($1,200-1,800/month for 1BR), car insurance ($150-250/month), groceries ($300-500/month), etc.

**DOK 3: What conclusions can you draw about how your career choice directly affects what kind of lifestyle you can afford in the DFW area?**

**EXIT TICKET: What is your career salary, your monthly net income, and your biggest expense category? Does your budget balance? (d(5)(D))**

### Day 3: Cost of Living Comparison

**WARM-UP: Do you think it costs more to live in Dallas, New York, or Los Angeles? Make a prediction.**

#### Cost of Living Research (35 min)

> **Teacher:** "Today we are comparing what life costs in DIFFERENT cities. The same career can pay different salaries in different places, and the cost of living varies enormously. A nurse in Dallas and a nurse in San Francisco might earn very different salaries, but their expenses are also very different. Let us find out who actually has more money left over."

Students complete the Cost of Living Comparison worksheet. They compare their chosen career's salary and expenses in DFW versus two other cities using CareerOneStop's cost of living comparison tool. Categories: housing, food, transportation, healthcare, and taxes. They calculate which city gives the most 'leftover' money after basic expenses.

**DOK 4: Based on your cost of living comparison, if you were offered a job in your career at $20,000 more per year in San Francisco versus staying in Dallas, would you take it? Use your data to justify your decision.**

**EXIT TICKET: Compare the cost of living for your career in DFW versus one other city. Which location leaves you with more money after expenses? (d(5)(A), d(5)(E))**

### Day 4: NGPF / EverFi Financial Literacy

**WARM-UP: What is the difference between saving and investing? Which one grows your money faster?**

#### Financial Literacy Supplement (35 min)

> **Teacher:** "The H&L MoneySkills module gave you the basics of budgeting. Today we are going deeper with a financial literacy program. You will learn about saving strategies, understanding credit, managing debt, and why starting to save early matters. These are life skills that apply to EVERY career."

Students complete a selected NGPF or EverFi financial literacy module. Options: NGPF's 'Spending and Saving' unit, EverFi's 'FutureSmart' financial literacy module, or Practical Money Skills' budgeting game. Teacher selects based on what is available and appropriate for the grade level.

#### Paying for College and Training (10 min)

> **Teacher:** "We have been talking about what life costs AFTER you start your career. But there is a cost BEFORE that: education and training. Let us talk about how you pay for it. FAFSA -- the Free Application for Federal Student Aid -- is the first step for ANY college student. Even if you think you will not qualify, you should fill it out because it opens the door to grants, which are free money you do not pay back. Scholarships are another source of free money, and there are thousands available based on grades, activities, community service, and even your career pathway. Work-study lets you work part-time on campus to help cover costs."

Class discussion: Teacher asks students to estimate the cost of 4 years of college versus a 2-year certification program versus an apprenticeship. Emphasize that FAFSA is free to complete and available to all students regardless of income level.

**DOK 3: What conclusions can you draw about why someone earning $50,000/year who saves 10% of their income from age 22 will have significantly more money at age 60 than someone who starts saving at age 35?**

**DOK 2: How would you compare the financial impact of paying for college with scholarships versus paying with student loans? (d(3)(C))**

**EXIT TICKET: Name one financial concept you learned today and one method for paying for postsecondary education. (d(5)(D), d(3)(C))**

### Day 5: Budget Presentations + A&C Cluster Wrap-Up

**WARM-UP: If you could give your future self ONE piece of financial advice, what would it be?**

#### Think-Pair-Share: Budget Comparison (15 min)

Students pair with someone who chose a DIFFERENT career for their budget. They compare: salaries, biggest expense, money left over, and overall lifestyle. Partners discuss what surprised them about the other person's budget.

#### A&C Cluster Final Wrap-Up (10 min)

> **Teacher:** "We are closing out the Architecture and Construction cluster and financial literacy. Over the past 5 weeks you explored architecture, engineering, construction, skilled trades, and now you know what real life costs. Next week we look at one more career area: real estate."

> [H&L PLATFORM] Students finalize all A&C favorites in H&L and ensure their Career Plan reflects this six weeks' exploration.

#### Salary Comparison Across the Year (15 min)

Students compare salaries of at least 3 careers across DIFFERENT clusters they explored this year (e.g., one from IT, one from Health Science, one from Trades). They create a quick summary chart showing salary, education time, and education cost for each.

**DOK 4: If you were creating a 'Career Value Score' that balances salary, education time, education cost, and job satisfaction, how would you weight each factor? Apply your scoring system to your 3 careers.**

**EXIT TICKET: Compare 3 careers you explored this year on salary and education. Which has the best overall value for YOU and why? (d(5)(E))**

Personal Budget + Cost of Living Comparison (Day 5): Students submit their completed personal budget and cost of living comparison worksheet. Scored on: budget accuracy and completeness (d(5)(D)), salary comparison across 3+ careers (d(5)(E)), and cost of living analysis quality (d(5)(A)).

## Differentiation

### Scaffolded Learning

- Provide a pre-filled budget example for one career so students can see the format before building their own.
- Offer a simplified budget template with fewer expense categories for students who need support.
- Provide DFW cost reference sheets with average rent, utility, and food costs so students do not need to research every number.

### Extensions

- Students calculate how long it would take to save for a down payment on a house on their career salary.
- Create a 'Financial Freedom Timeline' showing when they could be debt-free based on their career and budget choices.
- Compare budgets for the same career at three different experience levels (entry, mid, senior) to show salary growth impact.

### ELL Language Support

- Pre-teach: Budget = Presupuesto, Net Income = Ingreso neto, Fixed Expenses = Gastos fijos, Variable Expenses = Gastos variables, Cost of Living = Costo de vida.
- Bilingual budget template with Spanish category labels.
- Teacher modeling of the budget on the projector provides visual scaffolding before independent work.
- Pair ESL students with bilingual peers during the Think-Pair-Share budget comparison.
