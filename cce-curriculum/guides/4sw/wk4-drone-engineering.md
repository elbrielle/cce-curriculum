## Lesson Objective

Students will explore the STEM/Engineering career cluster through Hats & Ladders, investigate emerging drone and UAS (Unmanned Aerial Systems) occupations, research FAA Part 107 certification requirements, and operate classroom drones to demonstrate foundational flight skills and engineering principles.

## Demonstration of Learning

> "I can describe at least three emerging drone careers, explain the FAA Part 107 certification process, and demonstrate basic drone operation while connecting the experience to real-world engineering careers."

## TEKS Alignment

- **d(1)(D):** Research and evaluate emerging occupations related to career interest areas.
- **d(2)(A):** Research and describe applicable academic, technical, certification, and training requirements.
- **d(5)(B):** Classify occupations as high-skill, high-wage, and high-demand.

## Materials Needed

- Chromebooks with internet access
- Hats & Ladders student accounts
- Classroom drones (mini-drones or DJI Tello Education, 1 per team)
- FAA Drone Zone: https://www.faa.gov/uas
- BLS - Mechanical Engineers: https://www.bls.gov/ooh/architecture-and-engineering/mechanical-engineers.htm

- Printed UAS Career Research Template (1 per student)
- Safety briefing handout for drone operation

## Career Connection

Drones are transforming nearly every industry: agriculture (crop monitoring), construction (site surveys), real estate (aerial photography), emergency services (search and rescue), delivery (Amazon, UPS), filmmaking, and infrastructure inspection. The FAA projects the commercial drone market will continue rapid growth, making drone-related careers some of the most in-demand emerging occupations. At Irving High, students can earn the FAA Part 107 Remote Pilot certification, which is the license required to fly drones commercially. This directly connects to the TSA Drone Challenge that many of your students are already competing in.

**Irving ISD Pathway: Drone Engineering (Irving High) leads to FAA Part 107 certification. This pathway connects to engineering, aviation, and technology careers across multiple industries.**

## Vocabulary

- **UAS (Unmanned Aerial Systems):** The complete system that includes the drone, the controller, the communication link, and the software used to operate it.
- **FAA Part 107:** The federal certification required to operate drones commercially. It covers airspace rules, weather, emergency procedures, and regulations.
- **Geofencing:** Technology that creates virtual boundaries preventing drones from flying in restricted areas like airports or government buildings.
- **Payload:** The equipment carried by a drone beyond what is needed for flight, such as cameras, sensors, or delivery packages.
- **Emerging Occupation:** A career that is new, rapidly growing, or evolving due to technological changes. Drone-related careers are a prime example.

## Bridge to Theory (Hats & Ladders)

In H&L, the STEM and Engineering clusters contain career profiles for engineering and technology careers. While dedicated 'Drone Engineer' may be a newer title, students can explore related Hats such as Mechanical Engineer, Aerospace Engineer, Robotics Technician, and GIS Specialist. The key this week is d(1)(D) -- researching and evaluating EMERGING occupations. Students will use H&L career profiles as a starting point, then supplement with current industry data to research drone-specific roles that may not yet have established career profiles.


## IISD Instructional Strategies

- **Jigsaw:** For the UAS career research on Day 2, assign each team a different drone industry (agriculture, construction, filmmaking, delivery, emergency services). Teams become experts on their industry and then teach other groups, so every student learns about multiple applications.
- **Active Monitoring:** During drone flight days, circulate with a safety and engagement checklist: all team members rotate as pilot, all teams connect flying to career applications, and safety protocols are followed.

## Lesson Sequence

### Day 1: Engineering & Technology Pathway Exploration

**WARM-UP: Name 3 things you have seen a drone do (in person, on TV, or online).**

#### H&L STEM/Engineering Exploration (20 min)

> **Teacher:** "Today we are exploring the STEM and Engineering career cluster. This is the cluster for people who like to design, build, test, and improve things. Open H&L and navigate to the STEM or Engineering cluster. Explore careers like Mechanical Engineer, Aerospace Engineer, and Robotics Technician."

> [H&L PLATFORM] Students explore the Engineering cluster in H&L. The workbook (Ch 8) lists six pathways: Engineering Foundations, Geospatial Engineering and Land Surveying, Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering, Drone (Unmanned Vehicle) Engineer, Electrical Engineering, and Civil Engineering. Key workbook activities: "Protecting Wildlife" (engineering for conservation), "Mission to Mars" (space engineering challenge), and "Infrastructure Imagination" (civil engineering city infrastructure project). Students use the Hat Finder to explore Hats: Mechanical Engineer, Aerospace Engineer, Electrical Engineer, Drone Engineer, Robotics Technician.

#### Emerging UAS Occupations Discussion (20 min)

> **Teacher:** "The careers in H&L are mostly established occupations. But some of the fastest-growing careers are so NEW that they are still being defined. Drone Engineer, UAS Pilot, Drone Data Analyst, Drone Delivery Coordinator -- these jobs barely existed 10 years ago. That is what the TEKS call an emerging occupation. Today we start researching these."

Teacher introduces 5-6 emerging drone-related careers with brief descriptions. Students identify which H&L engineering careers connect most closely to each drone role.

**DOK 2: How would you describe the connection between a traditional Mechanical Engineer career and the newer role of Drone Engineer? What skills overlap?**

**EXIT TICKET: Name one established engineering career from H&L and one emerging drone career. How are they connected? (d(1)(D))**

### Day 2: UAS Industry Jigsaw Research

**WARM-UP: Pick an industry: farming, construction, movies, delivery, or emergency rescue. How could a drone help in that industry?**

#### Jigsaw Research (35 min)

> **Teacher:** "Each team has been assigned a different industry where drones are revolutionizing the work. Your job is to become the EXPERT on drones in your industry. Research: What do the drones do? What careers exist? What certifications are needed? What does it pay? How fast is this field growing? You will teach the rest of the class tomorrow."

Teams research their assigned drone industry using BLS, FAA.gov, DJI Education, and general web searches. They complete the UAS Career Research Template for their industry. Industries: Agriculture (crop monitoring, spraying), Construction (surveying, inspection), Film/Media (aerial cinematography), Delivery (logistics), Emergency Services (search and rescue, disaster response).

**DOK 4: Based on your research, what argument would you make for classifying your industry's drone careers as high-skill, high-wage, AND high-demand? Use salary, education, and growth data to support your argument.**

**EXIT TICKET: Name the drone career in your assigned industry with the highest salary and explain what training it requires. (d(2)(A), d(5)(B))**

### Day 3: Drone Safety + Flight Basics

**WARM-UP: Why do you think the FAA requires a certification to fly drones commercially? What could go wrong without regulations?**

#### FAA Part 107 Overview (15 min)

> **Teacher:** "Before anyone touches a drone, we need to talk about the FAA Part 107 certification. This is the law. If you want to fly a drone for ANY commercial purpose -- taking photos for a real estate company, inspecting a bridge, delivering a package -- you MUST have this certification. And guess what? You can start earning it at Irving High through the Drone Engineering pathway."

Teacher presents FAA Part 107 overview: what it covers (airspace, weather, regulations, emergency procedures), how to get it (study + 60-question exam at an FAA testing center), minimum age (16), and career impact. Students note requirements on their research template.

#### Drone Safety Briefing + Flight Practice (25 min)

Distribute safety briefing handouts. Cover: pre-flight checklist, battery safety, no-fly zones, emergency procedures. Then distribute classroom drones. Teams practice basic flight: takeoff, hover, controlled landing. Every team member must rotate as pilot.

**DOK 2: How would you describe the safety procedures you followed today and why each one matters for professional drone operations?**

Engineering Design Process: Define and Prototype -- Students learn basic flight controls as the foundational skill for the engineering challenge on Day 4.


**EXIT TICKET: List 3 things covered on the FAA Part 107 exam and explain why each one is important for safe drone operation. (d(2)(A))**

### Day 4: Drone Navigation Challenge

**WARM-UP: What was the hardest part about flying the drone yesterday? What will you do differently today?**

#### Engineering Challenge (35 min)

> **Teacher:** "Today is the drone engineering challenge. Your team has been hired to fly a drone through a navigation course -- like a real drone inspection mission. You need to take off, navigate through obstacles, hover at specific checkpoints, and land precisely on a target. This simulates what a real UAS Pilot does when inspecting a bridge or surveying a construction site."

Teacher sets up a simple indoor navigation course using cones, chairs, or tape targets. Teams fly their drone through the course. Each team member must complete at least one run. Teams track their accuracy scores and times, then strategize improvements.

**DOK 4: Based on your team's performance data, what specific improvements would you recommend, and what evidence supports each recommendation?**

Engineering Design Process: Test and Evaluate -- Teams fly the navigation course, collect data on accuracy and time, and iterate on their approach.


**EXIT TICKET: Describe one way you improved your drone flying between your first and last attempt today. Connect this to the Engineering Design Process. (d(1)(D))**

### Day 5: Jigsaw Presentations + Favorites

**WARM-UP: If you could start a drone business tomorrow, what service would you offer and who would your customers be?**

#### Jigsaw Presentations (25 min)

Each team presents their drone industry research to the class: industry overview, key drone careers, salary data, growth projections, and certification requirements. Class members take notes on all industries. 4 minutes per team.

#### H&L Favorites + Reflection (15 min)

> **Teacher:** "Go into H&L and favorite any engineering or technology careers that connect to what we learned this week. Think about how drone careers overlap with the traditional engineering careers you explored on Day 1. Also -- if you are in TSA Drone Challenge, think about how this week's learning connects to your competition experience."

> [H&L PLATFORM] Students favorite STEM/Engineering careers in H&L and update their Building Blocks with drone-related skills and experiences. (Ch 8: Engineering directs students to use the Hat Finder to explore and favorite Engineering careers; Ch 16: My Next Steps provides the Building Blocks template.)

**DOK 3: What conclusions can you draw about how participating in TSA Drone Challenge builds real career skills? Name at least two specific skills.**

**EXIT TICKET: Name the drone industry you found most interesting and one career within it that you would consider pursuing. Classify it as high-skill, high-wage, and/or high-demand. (d(1)(D), d(5)(B))**

## Formative Assessment

- H&L STEM cluster exploration completion (Day 1). (d(1)(D))
- UAS Career Research Template accuracy (Day 2). (d(2)(A), d(5)(B))
- Drone safety briefing knowledge check (Day 3). (d(2)(A))
- Active monitoring checklist during flight days (Days 3-4). (d(1)(D))

## Summative Assessment

UAS Jigsaw Presentation + Career Classification (Day 5): Teams present their industry research. Each student submits a written classification of their top drone career as high-skill, high-wage, and/or high-demand with supporting evidence. Scored on: emerging occupation research quality (d(1)(D)), certification knowledge (d(2)(A)), and accurate career classification (d(5)(B)).

## Differentiation

### Scaffolded Learning

- Provide pre-selected research sources for each industry to prevent students from getting lost in web searches.
- Offer simplified drone controls reference card with visual diagrams for students who struggle with flight.
- Allow teams to use a flight simulator app before operating physical drones.

### Extensions

- Students research and present on a drone career that does not yet exist but will in 5 years.
- Advanced pilots can attempt the navigation course with added complexity (time pressure, additional checkpoints).
- Students create a business plan for a drone services company, including startup costs and projected revenue.

### ELL Language Support

- Pre-teach: Drone = Dron, Certification = Certificacion, Emerging = Emergente, Payload = Carga util.
- Visual safety briefing handout with diagrams and bilingual labels.
- Jigsaw presentation can include visual slides to support verbal explanations.
- Pair ESL students with bilingual peers during research and presentation prep.
